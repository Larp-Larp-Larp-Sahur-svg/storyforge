THE LANTERN OF ASH

Kael had always believed the city of Vothmar would outlast him. It was older than the kings whose statues crumbled in its squares, older than the river Sael that split it into the High Ward and the Drowned Quarter. But on the night the lighthouse-temple went dark, Kael understood that even Vothmar could die.

He was a lampwright's apprentice, seventeen, with quick hands and a quicker tongue, and a habit of climbing where he should not. From the roof of the Guild he watched the great Lantern of Ash — the flame that had burned atop the temple for nine hundred years — gutter and fail. In the harbor below, ships lost their bearings. Somewhere a bell began to toll.

The High Cantor, Maela, found him before dawn. She was a severe woman, grey-eyed, who spoke to the flame as though it could answer. "The fire did not die," she told him. "It was taken. And only one who can climb unseen and lie convincingly can reach the one who took it." She pressed a cold iron key into his palm. "The Drowned Quarter remembers you, lampwright. Use that."

In the Drowned Quarter ruled Surch, a smuggler-prince with a hundred debts owed to him and a thousand owed by him. He kept a tame scholar named Ibrahe who had once mapped the temple's secret stairs. And beneath them both, in the flooded undercrofts, something patient waited — the thing that had swallowed the light, and hungered for more.

Kael had three things: the iron key, a knife he barely knew how to use, and the truth that the city's fate had somehow become his own. The river was rising. The dark was learning his name. And every choice from here would cost him something he could not yet count.
